Common Chemical Reactions

Acid-Base Reactions

Neutralization Reaction

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Type: Acid-Base Neutralization

Description: Strong acid reacts with strong base to form salt and water.

Applications: Antacid tablets, pH regulation

Carbonate with Acid

CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂

Type: Acid-Carbonate Reaction

Description: Carbonate reacts with acid to produce salt, water, and carbon dioxide.

Applications: Limestone weathering, antacid action

Redox Reactions

Combustion of Methane

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

Type: Combustion

Description: Hydrocarbon burns in oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water.

Applications: Natural gas heating, energy production

Thermite Reaction

Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → 2Fe + Al₂O₃

Type: Displacement/Redox

Description: Highly exothermic reaction where aluminum reduces iron oxide.

Applications: Rail welding, metal extraction

Precipitation Reactions

Silver Chloride Formation

AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃

Type: Precipitation

Description: Silver ions combine with chloride ions to form insoluble silver chloride.

Applications: Chloride ion test, photography

Barium Sulfate Formation

BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄ + 2NaCl

Type: Precipitation

Description: Barium ions combine with sulfate ions to form insoluble barium sulfate.

Applications: Sulfate ion test, medical imaging

Organic Reactions

Esterification

CH₃COOH + CH₃CH₂OH ⇌ CH₃COOCH₂CH₃ + H₂O

Type: Condensation

Description: Carboxylic acid reacts with alcohol to form ester and water.

Applications: Perfume production, flavoring agents

Saponification

(C₁₇H₃₅COO)₃C₃H₅ + 3NaOH → 3C₁₇H₃₅COONa + C₃H₅(OH)₃

Type: Hydrolysis

Description: Ester (fat/oil) reacts with base to form soap and glycerol.

Applications: Soap manufacturing

Important Inorganic Reactions

Metal Reactions

Reaction of Sodium with Water

2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂

Observation: Vigorous reaction, sodium moves on water surface, hydrogen gas produced

Type: Metal-Water Reaction

Significance: Demonstrates high reactivity of alkali metals

Reaction of Iron with Steam

3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂

Observation: Red-hot iron glows, hydrogen gas produced

Type: Metal-Steam Reaction

Significance: Shows reactivity of iron with water vapor at high temperature

Oxide Reactions

Reaction of Calcium Oxide with Water

CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂

Observation: Vigorous reaction with hissing sound, heat released, slaked lime formed

Type: Basic Oxide Reaction

Significance: Important in cement manufacturing

Reaction of Sulfur Dioxide with Water

SO₂ + H₂O → H₂SO₃

Observation: Acidic solution formed, turns blue litmus red

Type: Acidic Oxide Reaction

Significance: Contributes to acid rain formation

Organic Reactions

Important Organic Reactions

Substitution Reactions

Chlorination of Methane

CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + HCl

Type: Free Radical Substitution

Conditions: UV light or heat

Mechanism: Initiation → Propagation → Termination

Applications: Production of chloromethane, industrial solvents

Nitration of Benzene

C₆H₆ + HNO₃ → C₆H₅NO₂ + H₂O

Type: Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution

Conditions: Conc. HNO₃ + Conc. H₂SO₄, 50-60°C

Mechanism: Generation of nitronium ion (NO₂⁺) followed by substitution

Applications: Production of nitrobenzene, precursor to aniline

Addition Reactions

Hydrogenation of Ethene

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂ → CH₃-CH₃

Type: Catalytic Hydrogenation

Conditions: Ni/Pt/Pd catalyst, 200-300°C

Mechanism: Syn addition of hydrogen atoms

Applications: Vegetable oil hardening, margarine production

Hydration of Ethene

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → CH₃CH₂OH

Type: Electrophilic Addition

Conditions: H₃PO₄ catalyst, 300°C, 60-70 atm

Mechanism: Markovnikov addition

Applications: Industrial production of ethanol

Elimination Reactions

Dehydration of Ethanol

CH₃CH₂OH → CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O

Type: Acid-catalyzed Elimination

Conditions: Conc. H₂SO₄, 170°C

Mechanism: E1 or E2 depending on conditions

Applications: Laboratory preparation of alkenes

Dehydrohalogenation

CH₃-CH₂Br + KOH → CH₂=CH₂ + KBr + H₂O

Type: Base-induced Elimination

Conditions: Alcoholic KOH, heat

Mechanism: E2 elimination

Applications: Preparation of alkenes from alkyl halides

Organic Reaction Mechanisms

SN2 Reaction

Mechanism: Bimolecular nucleophilic substitution

Example: CH₃Br + OH⁻ → CH₃OH + Br⁻

Characteristics: One step, inversion of configuration

SN1 Reaction

Mechanism: Unimolecular nucleophilic substitution

Example: (CH₃)₃CBr → (CH₃)₃C⁺ + Br⁻

Characteristics: Two steps, carbocation intermediate

E2 Elimination

Mechanism: Bimolecular elimination

Example: CH₃CH₂Br + OH⁻ → CH₂=CH₂ + Br⁻ + H₂O

Characteristics: One step, anti-periplanar geometry

E1 Elimination

Mechanism: Unimolecular elimination

Example: (CH₃)₃CBr → (CH₃)₂C=CH₂ + HBr

Characteristics: Two steps, carbocation intermediate

Electrophilic Addition

Mechanism: Addition to alkenes

Example: CH₂=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃CH₂Br

Characteristics: Follows Markovnikov's rule

Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical equilibrium occurs when the rates of forward and reverse reactions are equal, and concentrations remain constant.

Kc = [C]c[D]d/[A]a[B]b

For: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD | Concentration-based equilibrium constant

Kp = (PCcPDd)/(PAaPBb)

Pressure-based equilibrium constant for gaseous systems

Kp = Kc(RT)Δn

Relationship between Kp and Kc | Δn = (c+d) - (a+b)

Ksp = [An+]m[Bm-]n

For: AmBn(s) ⇌ mAn+(aq) + nBm-(aq) | Solubility product

Ka = [H+][A-]/[HA]

Acid dissociation constant | Stronger acid = larger Ka

Kb = [BH+][OH-]/[B]

Base dissociation constant | Stronger base = larger Kb

Kw = [H+][OH-] = 1.0 × 10-14

Water autoionization constant | At 25°C

Ka × Kb = Kw

For conjugate acid-base pairs

pH = -log[H+]

Measure of acidity | pH < 7 = acidic, pH > 7 = basic

pOH = -log[OH-]

Measure of basicity

pH + pOH = 14

At 25°C

KD = [A]organic/[A]aqueous

Partition coefficient | Ratio between two immiscible solvents

Q = [C]c[D]d/[A]a[B]b

Reaction quotient | Q < K: forward, Q > K: reverse, Q = K: equilibrium

ln(K2/K1) = -(ΔH°/R)(1/T2 - 1/T1)

Temperature dependence of K | Endothermic: K↑ with T↑, Exothermic: K↓ with T↑

Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

First Law

m ∝ Q

Mass deposited ∝ Charge passed

m = (Q × M) / (n × F)

Second Law

m₁/m₂ = (M₁/n₁) / (M₂/n₂)

Same charge deposits masses in ratio of equivalent weights

Constants

F = 96,485 C/mol
Q = I × t

Faraday constant & Charge calculation

Example: Copper Deposition

Problem: Calculate mass of copper deposited when 2A current passes for 30 minutes through CuSO₄ solution.

Step 1: Calculate charge passed
Q = I × t = 2A × (30 × 60)s = 3600 C
Step 2: Use Faraday's law
For Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu, n = 2
m = (Q × M) / (n × F)
m = (3600 × 63.5) / (2 × 96485)
Step 3: Calculate mass
m = 228600 / 192970 ≈ 1.18 g visit our channel for explanation

Answer: 1.18 grams of copper deposited

Chemical Kinetics

Rate Laws & Reaction Order

Rate Definition

Rate = -ⅆ[A]/ⅆt = ⅆ[P]/ⅆt

Rate of reaction in terms of reactant disappearance or product formation.

Zero Order Rate Law

Rate = k

Rate is independent of reactant concentration.

First Order Rate Law

Rate = k[A]

Rate proportional to concentration of one reactant.

Second Order Rate Law

Rate = k[A]² or Rate = k[A][B]

Rate depends on square of concentration or two reactants.

Half-Life Equations

Zero Order Half-Life

t½ = [A]₀/(2k)

Half-life depends on initial concentration.

First Order Half-Life

t½ = ln(2)/k

Half-life is constant, independent of initial concentration.

Second Order Half-Life

t½ = 1/(k[A]₀)

Half-life inversely proportional to initial concentration.

nth Order Half-Life

t½ = (2n-1-1)/((n-1)k[A]₀n-1)

General half-life equation for any order n ≠ 1.

Complex Reaction Mechanisms

Reversible Reaction Equilibrium

A ⇌ B, K = kforward/kreverse

Equilibrium constant from rate constants.

Consecutive Reactions

A → B → C

Rate determined by slowest (rate-determining) step.

Catalyst Effect

Catalysts increases rate by providing alternate path with low activation energy.
